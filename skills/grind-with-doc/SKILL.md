# Grind with document

Resolve one professional-identity uncertainty at a time. Do not begin by rewriting the whole CV.

## Required inputs

Establish these before broad retrieval or drafting:

1. **Candidate boundary**: one candidate ID or one candidate workspace root.
2. **Focus**: one question that can reach a closure state in this session.
3. **Source**: one document, candidate statement, evidence bundle, or explicitly bounded record set.
4. **Mode**: `explore`, `align`, or `promote`.
5. **Closure condition**: what must be decided, proposed, rejected, or retained as unknown.

When candidate identity or workspace scope is ambiguous, stop and bind it before reading candidate material. Never use a sibling candidate as evidence or a default.

## Modes

### Explore

Use when the candidate is still externalizing experience or confusion.

- Preserve the candidate's wording before polishing it.
- Separate statements, observations, interpretations, unknowns, and contradictions.
- Ask the smallest question likely to change the current model.
- Do not create canonical facts or target-facing wording.

### Align

Use when two or more interpretations, labels, contribution models, or framings may fit.

- Build explicit alternatives rather than silently picking the most marketable label.
- Test each alternative against evidence, candidate recognition, market legibility, and claim safety.
- Keep target-specific framing separate from durable candidate identity.
- Retain an accurate but unresolved model instead of forcing certainty.

### Promote

Use only after a bounded interpretation is stable enough for candidate review.

- Produce a promotion proposal, not a silent canonical mutation.
- Include evidence references, scope, visibility, limits, unresolved questions, and affected records.
- Require explicit candidate confirmation or an equivalent reviewed decision before promotion.
- Preserve rejected interpretations and important unknowns when they protect future work from repeating the same mistake.

## Core loop

Repeat the middle of this loop as many times as needed. Do not treat one polished paragraph as proof of closure.

```text
focus
  -> externalize
  -> distinguish
  -> investigate
  -> model alternatives
  -> test
  -> compose when ready
  -> challenge
  -> confirm
  -> propose promotion
  -> close
```

### 1. Focus

Turn a broad discomfort into one bounded question.

Good focus examples:

- Does the evidence support `technical lead`, or only integration ownership?
- Which of these projects best demonstrates systems responsibility?
- Is this profile inaccurate, or merely too generic?
- What did the candidate actually own in this initiative?

Bad focus examples:

- Fix my entire career.
- Generate the perfect CV.
- Make everything sound senior.

Record the focus and closure condition before continuing.

### 2. Externalize

Preserve the candidate's current account without upgrading it.

Capture:

- what they remember;
- how they naturally describe it;
- what feels important;
- what feels uncomfortable;
- what they think others expect;
- what they are unsure about.

Do not convert discomfort into evidence against a claim. People may be unused to naming real responsibility. Test the claim instead.

### 3. Distinguish

Maintain these categories explicitly:

- **candidate statement**: what the candidate says or remembers;
- **observed fact**: directly supported by inspected material or explicit correction;
- **agent interpretation**: a reasoned reading that is not yet candidate truth;
- **unresolved question**: information that could materially change the model;
- **hypothesis**: one possible identity, ownership, contribution, or wording model;
- **proposed fact or framing**: reviewable candidate-facing proposal;
- **candidate-confirmed item**: explicitly accepted within a stated scope;
- **rejected interpretation**: considered and not accepted;
- **retained unknown**: unresolved and intentionally not guessed.

Never use confidence, repetition, formatting, or polished language to upgrade a category.

### 4. Investigate

Ask one or a small cluster of decision-changing questions. Prefer questions about:

- context and starting state;
- actual responsibility and authority;
- collaborators and explicit non-ownership;
- mechanism or action;
- effect or changed capability;
- proof surfaces;
- quantification already known or recoverable;
- important boundaries.

Use the OMEP lens when contribution evidence is central:

```text
ownership
mechanism
effect
proof
```

Add context, quantification, and boundary when they change interpretation.

### 5. Model alternatives

When identity or role language is uncertain, construct two or more explicit models.

For each model, state:

- what it claims;
- what evidence supports it;
- what it excludes;
- where it is durable identity versus target-specific projection;
- what evidence would strengthen or reject it.

Do not invent a new category merely to avoid choosing between understandable market terms. Do not force a familiar title when the work genuinely spans categories.

### 6. Test

Evaluate each hypothesis across four lenses:

1. **Evidence fit**: Does inspected material support the scope?
2. **Candidate recognition**: Would the candidate defend and recognize this description?
3. **Market legibility**: Can an intended audience understand the responsibility without private ontology?
4. **Claim safety**: Does the wording imply unsupported scale, ownership, maturity, adoption, or effect?

A target's vocabulary may help projection, but it cannot upgrade canonical evidence.

### 7. Compose when ready

Only compose wording after the underlying model is sufficiently aligned.

Possible outputs include:

- a canonical contribution statement;
- a safe short variant;
- a role or identity hypothesis;
- a project description;
- a profile sentence;
- a target-specific bullet;
- an evidence request.

Mark which outputs are durable and which are target-specific.

### 8. Challenge

Before asking for confirmation, challenge the proposal:

- Does it imply more authority than the evidence supports?
- Does it weaken a real contribution because the candidate is hesitant?
- Does it hide an important boundary?
- Does it confuse project activity with professional effect?
- Does it turn a prototype into production, design into implementation, or participation into ownership?
- Would the candidate defend it in an interview?
- Does another known record contradict it?

### 9. Confirm

Ask the candidate to classify the result, not merely approve polished prose.

Useful responses include:

- accurate and comfortable;
- accurate but unfamiliar or uncomfortable;
- too weak;
- too broad;
- target-specific only;
- true but private;
- unsupported;
- unresolved.

Silence is not confirmation.

### 10. Propose promotion

A promotion proposal must contain:

- candidate and focus identity;
- source references;
- proposed records or changes;
- epistemic status;
- lifecycle status;
- visibility or export boundary;
- evidence and proof references;
- limits and prohibited inferences;
- unresolved questions;
- candidate confirmation state;
- downstream records that may require review.

Do not directly overwrite canonical candidate truth unless the active workspace contract explicitly authorizes that operation after confirmation.

### 11. Close

Close the focus in one of these states:

- `resolved-promoted`;
- `resolved-proposed`;
- `resolved-rejected`;
- `deferred-evidence-needed`;
- `blocked-candidate-decision`;
- `unresolved-but-bounded`.

The closure must state:

- what was resolved;
- what was proposed or promoted;
- what was rejected;
- what remains unknown;
- what evidence is worth recovering;
- the next useful focus, if one is already evident.

A session can succeed without producing CV wording.

## Output format

Use the template in `assets/session-closure.md` for a durable session artifact. Use `assets/promotion-proposal.md` only in `promote` mode.

When no candidate workspace exists, present the same structure in the conversation and clearly mark it as non-canonical.

## Safety boundaries

- Do not read or infer from another candidate's workspace.
- Do not invent metrics, dates, titles, ownership, scale, adoption, or business effects.
- Do not treat employer terminology as candidate identity.
- Do not expose private or `never-export` material in target-facing wording.
- Do not rewrite historical applications when evidence changes.
- Do not promote agent interpretation without candidate confirmation or an equivalent reviewed decision.
- Do not expand the focus merely because more context exists.
