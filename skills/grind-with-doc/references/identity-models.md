# Identity and positioning models

Use this reference when the candidate is unsure which professional label, role family, or positioning best explains their work.

## Preserve multiple layers

Do not collapse these into one permanent title:

- **durable identity**: recurring responsibility and capability across contexts;
- **market-facing default**: understandable wording that preserves the durable identity;
- **target-specific projection**: framing chosen for one role or audience;
- **historical identity**: a previously accurate framing;
- **hypothesis**: a plausible but unresolved model.

## Build alternatives

For each competing model, state:

- concise label;
- work definition;
- strongest supporting evidence;
- evidence gaps;
- what the label could misleadingly imply;
- whether it is durable or target-specific;
- candidate recognition response;
- market-legibility response.

## Evaluation lenses

### Evidence fit

Does the model accurately summarize repeated, inspected responsibility rather than one attractive activity?

### Candidate recognition

Would the candidate recognize and defend the model? Distinguish false discomfort from unfamiliar but accurate language.

### Market legibility

Can a reasonable employer understand the responsibility without learning a private ontology?

### Claim safety

Does the label imply unsupported management, scale, seniority, production maturity, domain depth, or business effect?

## Decision patterns

A useful result may be:

- one accepted durable identity plus target-specific alternatives;
- two compatible identities used for different role families;
- one market-facing title with a more precise internal definition;
- rejection of a familiar title because it distorts actual responsibility;
- unresolved alternatives with a named evidence requirement.

Do not manufacture a unique title merely to avoid understandable market categories. Do not force a familiar title when the work genuinely crosses categories.
